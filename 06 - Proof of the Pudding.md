Chapter 0 had a lock that all mass-squared terms for physical fields are taken positive (or zero if massless) unless specifically discussing instability scenarios – clearly UGFT obeys this by construction (and if $m\_S \\to 0$ one enters a gauge theory regime with $S\_\\mu$ perhaps seen as a gauge field, but that’s a separate slice to consider with ghosts; MGSP1 chooses $m\_S>0$ for P1).
*   The interaction term $-,g\_S,S\_\\mu J\_5^\\mu$ is the unique minimal coupling between $S\_\\mu$ and fermions. Several points of verification: (i) **Consistency with Locks:** Chapter 2 explicitly locked “minimal torsion coupling is axial only”. That is precisely what we see – only $\\bar\\psi\\gamma^\\mu\\gamma^5\\psi$ couples to $S\_\\mu$, no vector or scalar torsion couplings are present. This respects parity: $J\_5^\\mu$ is pseudoscalar under parity, $S\_\\mu$ is pseudovector (axial); their product is parity-even so it can appear in the Lagrangian without violating symmetry. By contrast, a coupling $S\_\\mu \\bar\\psi\\gamma^\\mu\\psi$ (vector current) would be parity-even current times axial field (parity-odd), giving a parity-odd Lagrangian term – not forbidden _per se_ (parity is not a fundamental symmetry in nature), but it was likely disallowed by the “locks” to avoid mixing with the trace torsion $t\_\\mu$. Indeed $t\_\\mu$ would couple to $\\bar\\psi\\gamma^\\mu\\psi$ (vector charge current) if one tried, but MGSP1 keeps $t\_\\mu$ non-dynamical. So the chosen coupling isolates $S\_\\mu$ to the axial current, consistent with the idea that $S\_\\mu$ is the axial part of torsion. (ii) **Renormalizability:** The mass dimension of $S\_\\mu$ in 4D is 1 (same as a gauge field), $\\dim(J\_5^\\mu)=3$ (since $\\psi$ has dim $3/2$), so $g\_S$ is dimensionless $\\dim(g\_S)=0$. Any other torsion coupling (e.g. pseudoscalar $\\bar\\psi i\\gamma^5 \\psi$ times something, or tensor couplings) would either be non-renormalizable or violate a lock (for instance, a term like $q\_{\\mu\\nu\\rho}\\bar\\psi \\sigma^{\\mu\\nu}\\psi$ has higher dimension). So this choice passes the “renormalizability” and “no ghost” criteria (no derivative coupling, so no higher-derivative ghosts introduced). (iii) **Euler–Lagrange check:** This interaction will produce the correct source terms in both the $S\_\\mu$ equation **and** the Dirac equation. Varying $S\_\\mu$ gives $-g\_S J\_5^\\mu$ in the equations of motion (we will derive below that $\\nabla\_\\nu H^{\\nu\\mu}+m\_S^2 S^\\mu = g\_S J\_5^\\mu$). Varying $\\bar\\psi$ gives an extra term $-g\_S \\gamma^\\mu\\gamma^5 S\_\\mu \\psi$ in the Dirac equation, i.e. $(i!\\not D - m\_\\psi - g\_S \\gamma^\\mu\\gamma^5 S\_\\mu)\\psi=0$. So the coupling is internally consistent. (iv) **Coupling sign:** The sign $-g\_S S\_\\mu J\_5^\\mu$ was a choice; if we had $+g\_S$, it would simply flip the sign convention for $J\_5$. Physical results depend only on $g\_S^2$ for most predictions (like force strengths), so this is not an issue. The chosen sign likely correlates with defining $J\_5^\\mu$ as $\\bar\\psi\\gamma^\\mu\\gamma^5\\psi$ (not its negative). We won’t dwell on this since it doesn’t affect energy positivity or conservation directly (it will affect whether a positive $S\_0$ source gives an attractive or repulsive spin–spin potential, but that’s just convention).

Finally, we note the **principal symbol** of the $S\_\\mu$ kinetic operator is clean: since $H\_{\\mu\\nu}$ uses the Levi-Civita connection only (no torsion in $\\nabla$ acting on $S$), there is no direct $S$–gravity kinetic mixing at leading order. The text notes this design: $S\_\\mu$ is treated as a “matter 1-form” so that its kinetic term does not contain the affine torsion that would mix with the metric’s kinetic term. If we had used a full covariant derivative including contorsion $K\_{\\mu\\nu}{}^\\rho$ in $H\_{\\mu\\nu}$, the linearized equations would mix $h\_{\\mu\\nu}$ and $S\_\\mu$ fields (leading to difficulties in diagonalizing the propagator and potential ghost modes in the spin-2 channel). UGFT avoids that by using the _metric-compatible_ $\\nabla$ (which has Christoffel symbols but not contorsion; metric-affine connection is reduced by a Chapter 2 lock $Q\_{\\alpha\\mu\\nu}=0$ meaning no non-metricity). Thus $H\_{\\mu\\nu}$ is simply $2\\partial\_{\[\\mu} S\_{\\nu\]}$ on shell of metric compatibility. Our check: did this choice ruin any gauge symmetry? Not at all, since $S\_\\mu$ is not gauge (it’s massive). Instead, it simply means torsion’s _dynamical_ part $S\_\\mu$ is treated similarly to a $U(1)$ field but with gravity’s minimal coupling (just through $\\sqrt{|g|}$ factor and index moving by $g\_{\\mu\\nu}$). This gives a well-behaved propagator: indeed Chapter 2 records the momentum-space propagator $D\_{\\mu\\nu}(k)=\\frac{-g\_{\\mu\\nu} + \\frac{k\_\\mu k\_\\nu}{m^2}}{k^2 - m^2 + i0^+}$, which is the standard Proca form – no unusual $k\_\\mu k\_\\nu/k^2$ leftover (that piece is canceled by the $k\_\\mu k\_\\nu/m^2$ term, ensuring only physical polarizations propagate). **4.4.2 Ledger Terms – Boundary Pairing and Topological Integers:** The action includes $S\_{\\rm ledger}$, which collects all terms that do not contribute to bulk equations of motion:

S\_{\\rm ledger} \\;\\equiv\\; S\_{\\rm GHY}\[g\] \\;+\\; S\_{\\partial,\\text{matter/YM}}^{\\rm (BC)} \\;+\\; S\_{\\rm topo}\\,. \\tag{4.4.3}

This is spelled out in the text and we verify each piece with references to Chapters 0 and 3:

*   **$S\_{\\rm GHY}\[g\]$: Boundary pairing for gravity.** As already noted, $S\_{\\rm GHY}=\\frac{1}{8\\pi G}\\int\_{\\partial\\mathcal M} \\sqrt{|h|},K$. This is exactly the term we encountered in §4.3 and is required to cancel the boundary variation of the Einstein–Hilbert term. It ensures the metric variation is well-posed when fixing the induced metric $h\_{ij}$ on $\\partial\\mathcal M$. In the energy accounting, the _asymptotic value_ of $S\_{\\rm GHY}$ yields the ADM energy at spatial infinity. **Crucial check:** $S\_{\\rm GHY}$ is a total divergence term (depends only on boundary curvature $K$) – thus it does not alter the Einstein field equations in the bulk, satisfying MBLC. Our Chapter 3 ledger rules confirm this: the boundary term is of the form required by the “well-posed variation” and enters the energy ledger as the ADM “coin on the rim”. We will later use the fact that adding $S\_{\\rm GHY}$ doesn’t change $G\_{\\mu\\nu}$, but does allow us to derive a finite energy via the Hamiltonian constraint. The text explicitly highlights that $S\_{\\rm GHY}$’s variation cancels the metric’s boundary term and that its asymptotic value is the ADM surface energy, confirming our understanding. This aligns with Chapter 1’s Stop-5 (Boundary/Uniqueness), which requires that “variational surface terms \[be\] cancelled by compensators” (GHY here is essentially a compensator for the metric variation) and that any differences in boundary conditions are recorded only in the ledger.
*   **$S\_{\\partial,\\text{matter/YM}}^{(BC)}$: Matter and gauge boundary terms.** Depending on what boundary conditions are chosen for fields (Dirichlet vs Neumann), additional surface terms might be required for a well-posed variational principle. For example, for a scalar field with Neumann (derivative) boundary condition, one might add $-\\tfrac{1}{2}\\int\_{\\partial\\mathcal M} \\sqrt{|h|},\\phi,n^\\mu\\partial\_\\mu \\phi$ so that $\\delta \\phi$ on the boundary yields the correct condition (this is analogous to the electromagnetic case, etc.). UGFT doesn’t enumerate each case here, but states _“the specific surface term for each sector is included according to the declared BCs (Dirichlet/Neumann/Robin/Sommerfeld)”_. **Check:** This is exactly what one would expect from field theory textbooks and is also covered by Chapter 1’s boundary sentry (C1.6c) which demands no “boundary ghosts” – i.e. adding boundary counterterms if needed to keep energy non-negative at the boundary. Including these terms in $S\_{\\rm ledger}$ means they do not alter bulk equations (they are either boundary integrals or total divergences), satisfying our MBLC: _the ledger entries can change surface fluxes but never bulk dynamics_. We will assume these terms are correctly chosen such that, for instance, when we impose outgoing wave (Sommerfeld) conditions for radiation, the action is adjusted to reflect that (though Sommerfeld is more a condition than an action term, the uniqueness of solutions given that condition is part of Stop-5 which UGFT checks). The key point is that _any difference in how a problem is posed at the boundary does not change the _bulk_ field equations or propagators – it only shifts which ledger term carries the flux._ We will see an example in §4.12: eliminating $S\_\\mu$ yields an effective boundary operator (a Dirichlet-to-Neumann map) which _enters the ledger_ and does not alter the bulk solution.
*   **$S\_{\\rm topo}$: Topological invariant terms (ledger-only).** UGFT explicitly mentions: Pontryagin number $\\nu\_G=\\frac{1}{8\\pi^2}\\int \\mathrm{Tr}(F\\wedge F)\\in \\mathbb{Z}$ for gauge fields, Chern–Simons terms with rescaled $\\mathcal A = gA$ so that the level $k$ is manifestly integer, and Nieh–Yan/Holst terms in the gravitational/torsional sector. These are _all_ placed in the ledger. **Verification:** Chapter 3 provided the rationale (Descent Principle): these terms carry integer charges and do not contribute to local equations of motion. For example, the Yang–Mills Pontryagin term $F\\wedge F$ is a total derivative ($d,\\mathrm{Tr}(A\\wedge F - \\frac{g}{3}A\\wedge A\\wedge A)$) so adding it multiplies the action by an integer $8\\pi^2 \\nu\_G$ and does not affect $D\_\\nu F^{\\nu\\mu}=J^\\mu$. Likewise, the Holst term $e^a\\wedge e^b\\wedge \*R\_{ab}$ (with Immirzi parameter) and Nieh–Yan $d(e^a\\wedge T\_a) - e^a\\wedge e^b\\wedge R\_{ab}$ are known not to alter field equations in the absence of fermions (Holst only matters if one introduces Ashtekar variables; Nieh–Yan is proportional to a topological invariant if torsion has no propagating part except axial which we keep gauge-fixed). The ledger statement in the text confirms: _“all topological pieces contribute to integer ledgers and do not modify bulk EOM”_. This satisfies the **MLCP**: in 4D, forms of degree 4 (e.g. $F\\wedge F$ integrals) or effectively degree 3 (Chern–Simons is 3-form in 3D boundary) are purely global. We check that indeed UGFT does not propagate any new degree of freedom from these terms – e.g. no second-order dynamics from Holst’s term because it’s like $^\*RR$ (which in the first-order formalism just shifts the definition of spin connection slightly, equivalent to Immirzi parameter, but no new eq of motion). So **our audit confirms these terms are properly relegated to the ledger**: they are included in $S\_{\\rm topo}$ to track quantized charges (topological sectors $k, \\nu\_G, n\_{\\rm NY}$) but they never appear in $\\delta S\_{\\rm UGFT}/\\delta(\\text{field})$ for any local field. This is fully consistent with Chapter 0’s statement that integer ledgers are unchanged under Wick rotation, meaning they are topological invariants not affecting local fluctuations (another check: UGFT’s Wick map does not alter these integer values, confirming they don’t depend on continuous deformation – exactly the nature of topological terms).
*   **Duality Guard & No Double-Count:** The ledger content includes a note: _“in 4D, $p\\ge3$ forms have no local DOF – treat as ledger-only. For $B\\leftrightarrow \\theta$, propagate exactly one representative and impose duality by constraint to avoid double counting.”_. This is basically the MLCP plus a duality rule. **4.4.3 Field Equations from the Axial Sector (MGSP1 Enforcement):** Varying $S\_\\mu$ in the bulk action $\\int \\mathcal L\_S$ yields the Euler–Lagrange equation. Let’s derive it explicitly to ensure each term contributes correctly (and cite the given result to confirm):

Starting from $\\mathcal L\_S = -\\frac{1}{4}H\_{\\rho\\sigma}H^{\\rho\\sigma} + \\frac{1}{2}m\_S^2 S\_\\rho S^\\rho - g\_S S\_\\rho J\_5^\\rho$, treat $H\_{\\mu\\nu}$ as $\\nabla\_\\mu S\_\\nu - \\nabla\_\\nu S\_\\mu$. Variation $\\delta S\_\\alpha$ appears in $H\_{\\mu\\nu}$ as $\\delta H\_{\\mu\\nu} = 2\\nabla\_{\[\\mu} \\delta S\_{\\nu\]} = \\nabla\_\\mu \\delta S\_\\nu - \\nabla\_\\nu \\delta S\_\\mu$. Thus, the $-\\frac{1}{4}H^2$ part contributes  
 $-\frac{1}{2}H^{\mu\nu} \delta H_{\mu\nu} = -\frac{1}{2}H^{\mu\nu}(\nabla_\mu \delta S_\nu - \nabla_\nu \delta S_\mu) = - H^{\mu\nu}\nabla_\mu \delta S_\nu$   
(using antisymmetry of $H^{\\mu\\nu}$ to combine terms). Integrating by parts, this gives $\\nabla\_\\mu H^{\\mu\\nu},\\delta S\_\\nu$ (the boundary term $\\int \\delta S\_\\nu, H^{\\mu\\nu} n\_\\mu$ at $\\partial\\mathcal M}$ is canceled by the corresponding piece we included in $S\_{\\partial}^{(BC)}$ if we are fixing $S$ or its normal derivative — ensuring again the variation is well-posed). So from the kinetic term we get **(i)** $\\nabla\_\\mu H^{\\mu\\nu}$ in the EOM. The $+\\frac{1}{2}m\_S^2 S^2$ term varies to $+ m\_S^2 S^\\nu \\delta S\_\\nu$, contributing **(ii)** $m\_S^2 S^\\nu$. The $-g\_S S\_\\mu J\_5^\\mu$ term gives $-g\_S J\_5^\\nu \\delta S\_\\nu$, contributing **(iii)** $-g\_S J\_5^\\nu$. Setting the variation to zero, we obtain the equation:  
 $\nabla_\mu H^{\mu\nu} + m_S^2 S^\nu - g_S J_5^\nu = 0\,.$   
In index-free form, $\\nabla\_\\mu H^{\\mu\\nu} + m\_S^2 S^\\nu = g\_S J\_5^\\nu$. This is exactly the **Proca equation with axial source** given in the text:

\\nabla\_\\nu H^{\\nu\\mu} + m\_S^2 S^\\mu \\;=\\; g\_S\\,J\_5^\\mu\\,. \\tag{4.4.4}

This matches the MFES entry for the axial torsion and is the expected wave equation (a Maxwell-Proca equation) for $S\_\\mu$. We verify a few properties: (a) It is **hyperbolic**: in a local Lorentz frame, $\\nabla\_\\nu H^{\\nu\\mu} = \\partial\_\\nu H^{\\nu\\mu}$ (since Christoffel cancels in the antisymmetry), so it becomes $\\partial\_\\nu\\partial^\\nu S^\\mu - \\partial^\\mu(\\partial\\cdot S) + m^2 S^\\mu = g\_S J\_5^\\mu$. Taking another divergence yields $\\partial\_\\mu S^\\mu = \\frac{g\_S}{m\_S^2}\\partial\_\\mu J\_5^\\mu$ (assuming $m\_S\\neq0$) – which is exactly the secondary “constraint” equation shown in the text. In curved space covariant form, they write:

\\nabla\_\\mu S^\\mu \\;=\\; \\frac{g\_S}{m\_S^2}\\,\\nabla\_\\mu J\_5^\\mu\\,. \\tag{4.4.5}

If $J\_5$ is conserved (classically $\\nabla\_\\mu J\_5^\\mu=0$ for massless fermions), then this constraint implies $\\nabla\_\\mu S^\\mu = 0$. Thus _$S\_\\mu$ is transverse on-shell_, carrying 3 polarization DOF, not 4 – consistent with a massive spin-1 field. If $J\_5$ has a nonzero divergence (massive fermions or anomaly), $S\_\\mu$’s longitudinal part adjusts algebraically to compensate, as the text notes (“algebraic longitudinal”). In either case, no extra propagating mode sneaks in – this is a crucial consistency check (Stop-2: constraint closure). We see that constraint arise from $\\nabla\_\\mu$ acting on (4.4.4): $\\nabla\_\\mu\\nabla\_\\nu H^{\\nu\\mu} + m^2\\nabla\_\\mu S^\\mu = g\_S \\nabla\_\\mu J\_5^\\mu$. But $\\nabla\_\\mu\\nabla\_\\nu H^{\\nu\\mu}=0$ identically because $H^{\\nu\\mu}$ is antisymmetric and $\\nabla\_\\mu\\nabla\_\\nu$ commuted yields $R\_{\\mu\\nu}$ terms which contracted with antisymmetric $H^{\\nu\\mu}$ give zero (same reason $\\partial\_\\mu\\partial\_\\nu F^{\\nu\\mu}=0$ in Maxwell). Thus $m\_S^2 \\nabla\_\\mu S^\\mu = g\_S \\nabla\_\\mu J\_5^\\mu$, yielding (4.4.5). If initially $\\Gamma\\equiv \\nabla\_\\mu S^\\mu - \\frac{g\_S}{m\_S^2}\\nabla\_\\mu J\_5^\\mu$ is zero (constraint satisfied on some Cauchy surface), then $\\partial\_t \\Gamma = 0$ by this equation, so the constraint is preserved in time (closure). This satisfies Ghost-Stop 2: the Dirac constraint algorithm closes and the DOF count is consistent. We have 4 canonical $S\_\\mu$ components with one constraint (and one associated secondary, here it’s the same equation), no gauge invariance (Proca is not gauge), so DOF $=\\frac{1}{2}(8 - 2\\times1 - 0)=3$ physical, matching a massive spin-1.

4.5 Miller Field Equation Set (MFES) – Full Classical System
------------------------------------------------------------

In §4.5, the disparate field equations from gravity, $S\_\\mu$, gauge, and matter are collected into the **Miller Field Equation Set (MFES)**, which represents the complete classical equations of UGFT. Our task here is to check that each equation in this set corresponds exactly to the variation of $S\_{\\rm UGFT}$ and is mutually consistent. The MFES as given (schematically) is:

$$
\begin{aligned} G_{\mu\nu} &= 8\pi G\,T^{\rm total}_{\mu\nu},\\ \nabla_\nu H^{\nu\mu} + m_S^2 S^\mu &= g_S\,J_5^\mu, \qquad H_{\mu\nu}\equiv \nabla_\mu S_\nu - \nabla_\nu S_\mu,\\ D_\nu F^{\nu\mu} &= J^\mu_{\rm gauge},\\ \Box\phi - V'(\phi) &= 0,\\ (i\gamma^\mu D_\mu - m_\psi - g_S \gamma^\mu\gamma^5 S_\mu)\psi &= 0~, \end{aligned} 
$$

where $T^{\\rm total}_{\\mu\\nu} = T^{\\rm (grav)}_{\\mu\\nu} + T^{\\rm (YM)}_{\\mu\\nu} + T^{(\\phi)}_{\\mu\\nu} + T^{(\\psi)}_{\\mu\\nu} + T^{(S)}_{\\mu\\nu}$ is the sum of stress–energies of all sectors (note: $T^{\\rm (grav)}_{\\mu\\nu}$ here refers to the effective stress–energy one sometimes assigns to gravity when bringing $G_{\\mu\\nu}$ to the other side, but in GR we normally keep it as geometry – UGFT keeps $G\_{\\mu\\nu}$ on LHS, so $T^{\\rm total}$ includes all _non-gravitational_ fields; anyway the $G\_{\\mu\\nu}$ equation is correct).

We have essentially derived or checked each of these in previous sections, but let’s systematically verify alignment:

*   **Einstein Equation:** $G\_{\\mu\\nu} = 8\\pi G,T^{\\rm total}_{\\mu\\nu}$. This is exactly what varying the metric yields (including $S$ field’s stress–energy). In UGFT, $T^{\\rm total}_{\\mu\\nu}$ now includes the contribution from $S\_\\mu$ as well. We should ensure that the inclusion of $S$ does not spoil $\\nabla^\\mu G\_{\\mu\\nu}=0$ or energy conservation: it does not – since the $S$-equation ensures $\\nabla^\\mu T^{(S)}_{\\mu\\nu} = -\\nabla^\\mu (T^{\\rm matter}_{\\mu\\nu}+T^{\\rm YM}_{\\mu\\nu}+T^{(\\phi)}_{\\mu\\nu}+T^{(\\psi)}_{\\mu\\nu})$ if any, and in total $\\nabla^\\mu T^{\\rm total}_{\\mu\\nu}=0$ by Bianchi identity as noted. So Einstein’s equation holds and implies local conservation of combined stress–energy, consistent with Noether’s theorem for diffeomorphism invariance (Chapter 3 had this as part of “Stops” pipeline too: energy–momentum is conserved on shell).
*   **Axial Torsion Equation:** $\\nabla\_\\nu H^{\\nu\\mu} + m\_S^2 S^\\mu = g\_S J\_5^\\mu$. This is exactly the field equation we derived in §4.4, and we already found it to be consistent. We cross-check the **Dirac Equation** below to ensure $J\_5^\\mu$ in this equation is indeed the _same_ $J\_5^\\mu$ as defined by matter variation. Since $J\_5^\\mu \\equiv \\bar\\psi \\gamma^\\mu\\gamma^5 \\psi$ is the Noether current from the Dirac action for chiral phase rotations, and we added $-g\_S S\_\\mu \\bar\\psi \\gamma^\\mu\\gamma^5\\psi$ to the Lagrangian, variation w.rt $\\bar\\psi$ yields the Dirac eq with $-g\_S \\gamma^\\mu\\gamma^5 S\_\\mu$ term on the left, which means $g\_S \\gamma^\\mu\\gamma^5 S\_\\mu \\psi$ on the right. If we take the Dirac eq to the form $i\\gamma^\\mu D\_\\mu \\psi = m\_\\psi \\psi + g\_S \\gamma^\\mu \\gamma^5 S\_\\mu \\psi$, multiply by $\\bar\\psi$ and $\\gamma^5$, and take gamma matrix traces, we can derive a continuity equation $\\nabla\_\\mu J\_5^\\mu = 0$ (if $m\_\\psi=0$) or $\\propto 2im\_\\psi \\bar\\psi \\gamma^5 \\psi$ (if massive). However, since UGFT is classical and presumably taking $m\_\\psi$ possibly nonzero, $\\nabla\_\\mu J\_5^\\mu = 2im\_\\psi \\bar\\psi \\gamma^5\\psi$ which is nonzero in general – and indeed our $S$ equation can accommodate $\\nabla\\cdot J\_5 \\neq 0$ by $\\nabla\\cdot S$ adjusting. Everything is consistent: if $m\_\\psi\\neq0$, then chiral current is not conserved and $S$ picks up a longitudinal $\\nabla\\cdot S = \\frac{g\_S}{m^2}\\nabla\\cdot J\_5$ to satisfy its constraint. If $m\_\\psi=0$, then $\\nabla\\cdot J\_5 =0$ and automatically $\\nabla\\cdot S=0$ from (4.4.5), showing consistency. The coupling $g\_S$ appears in both this $S$ equation and the Dirac equation, ensuring energy exchange between the $S$ field and spinors is accounted for. In the stress–energy conservation, this manifests as $S\_\\mu$ doing work on the axial current and vice versa, but the sum is conserved. (We might recall Noether’s theorem: gauge invariance of $S$ (if $m\_S=0$) would enforce $\\nabla\_\\mu J\_5^\\mu=0$ needed for consistency, but here $m\_S\\neq0$ explicitly breaks that gauge invariance, so $\\nabla\\cdot J\_5$ need not be zero – which is consistent with fermion mass breaking chiral symmetry. So no contradiction.) Thus the axial torsion equation in MFES is correct and consistent with the matter equation.
*   **Yang–Mills Equation:** $D\_\\nu F^{\\nu\\mu} = J^\\mu\_{\\rm gauge}$. We already verified this in §4.3 (Maxwell/YM variation). In the MFES context, $J\_{\\rm gauge}^\\mu$ might include contributions from all charged matter (e.g. Dirac current $\\bar\\psi \\gamma^\\mu T^A \\psi$ for each gauge generator $A$) plus possibly an axial piece if $S$ had any charge (it doesn’t – $S$ is gauge-neutral in this setup). Since $S\_\\mu$ couples only axially to fermions, it does not contribute to gauge current directly. So $J\_{\\rm gauge}^\\mu$ is the same as in the legacy theory. There is no modification of the Maxwell/YM equation by $S$, and that’s consistent: UGFT did not unify in the sense of mixing gauge fields with torsion; it simply added a new $U(1)$ (in fact not even a gauge $U(1)$, just a Proca field) that couples to spin. So the gauge field equation remains standard and is sourced by electric/color charges and currents only. We check conservation: $D\_\\mu D\_\\nu F^{\\nu\\mu} = 0$ (by antisymmetry and Bianchi $D\_{\[\\mu}F\_{\\nu\\rho\]}=0$), so $D\_\\mu J^\\mu\_{\\rm gauge}=0$ – which is local charge conservation (and indeed is implied by matter Dirac equation). All good.
*   **Scalar Equation:** $\\Box \\phi - V'(\\phi)=0$. Verified in §4.3. In MFES, $V'(\\phi)$ may include interactions but $S$ field does not couple to scalars in this minimal UGFT extension, so the scalar equation is untouched by $S$. That means a spin-0 particle is oblivious to torsion in this theory (which is fine; only spin-1/2 couple to axial torsion minimally). The consistency requires that the scalar $T^{(\\phi)}_{\\mu\\nu}$ is still conserved; since it only couples to gravity (minimally), $\\nabla^\\mu T^{(\\phi)}_{\\mu\\nu}=0$ separately when $\\phi$ EOM holds. That plus other sectors’ conservation yields total conservation. Nothing to fix here.
*   **Dirac Equation (with axial coupling):** $(i\\gamma^\\mu D\_\\mu - m\_\\psi - g\_S\\gamma^\\mu\\gamma^5 S\_\\mu)\\psi=0$. This we already reasoned out from the action. It’s explicitly listed in MFES to show how $S\_\\mu$ enters as an extra term. Varying $\\bar\\psi$ yields $(i\\gamma^\\mu D\_\\mu + \\cdots - g\_S\\gamma^\\mu\\gamma^5 S\_\\mu)\\psi=0$. Actually careful: Variation $\\delta \\bar\\psi$ of $\\bar\\psi\\gamma^\\mu D\_\\mu \\psi$ gives $i\\gamma^\\mu D\_\\mu \\psi$; variation of $-g\_S S\_\\mu \\bar\\psi \\gamma^\\mu\\gamma^5 \\psi$ gives $-g\_S S\_\\mu \\gamma^\\mu \\gamma^5 \\psi$ in the equation. Multiply by $-1$ (just convention), we get $(i\\gamma^\\mu D\_\\mu - m\_\\psi - g\_S \\gamma^\\mu\\gamma^5 S\_\\mu)\\psi=0$, which matches the text exactly. This confirms no sign error. That’s consistent with the $S$-equation we have ($\\nabla\_\\nu H^{\\nu\\mu} + m^2 S^\\mu = g\_S J\_5^\\mu$ has a + sign on RHS). So all coupling signs are self-consistent: the exchange of energy between $S$ and spinor has opposite signs in their respective equations as expected (action-reaction). We also cross-check that if $g\_S\\to0$, the Dirac eq reduces to $(i!\\not D - m)\\psi=0$ (original Dirac) and $S$ eq becomes $\\nabla H + m^2 S=0$ (Proca with no source), which yields $S=0$ for appropriate boundary conditions – consistent decoupling. If $m\_S\\to0$ and $g\_S$ fixed, the Dirac eq becomes $(i!\\not D - m\_\\psi - g\_S\\gamma^\\mu\\gamma^5 S\_\\mu)\\psi=0$ with $S$ now effectively a massless axial “photon”; that system would have a gauge symmetry if anomalies ignored. But we keep $m\_S>0$.

We have therefore verified that **every equation in the MFES is exactly obtained from the unified action’s variation** and respects all prior definitions. The coupling terms ($g\_S$) appear in the $S$ and $\\psi$ equations in matching ways, and nowhere else (no coupling to gauge or scalar, as per our checks). This closure of the system means one can, in principle, solve these simultaneously for a given physical scenario. The text’s purpose in MFES is to show UGFT as a single set of equations akin to Einstein–Maxwell–Dirac plus one extra equation for $S$. Our audit finds no discrepancies in that representation. In addition, by summing the Bianchi identity and the divergence of each matter equation multiplied by field variables (Noether currents), one can show $\\nabla^\\mu T^{\\rm total}_{\\mu\\nu}=0$ – which we know is true given each sector’s local conservation. Section 4.6 conducts a detailed audit of each sector’s stress–energy tensor and verifies that each contributes **non-negative energy density** in local inertial frames (pointwise positivity), and that any potential ambiguities (like adding improvement terms or choosing different “lanes” for energy calculation) do not spoil the positivity or conservation – they only shift ledger entries. **Gravity (ADM Energy):** In GR, the local stress–energy pseudo-tensor for gravity can be defined (e.g. Einstein’s pseudotensor or Landau-Lifshitz), but UGFT avoids introducing a local gravitational $T\_{\\mu\\nu}$ in bulk – instead, gravitational energy is entirely encoded in the boundary ADM term. We have already discussed gravitational boundary term’s role: it’s crucial to making sure energy is counted properly but does not introduce negativity if done right. So for gravity, the audit is to check that we haven’t lost or double-counted any piece: we see that they identify _all_ gravitational energy with ADM (no local $T^{(g)}_{00}$ in the jar, consistent with general covariance which precludes a local gravitational energy density). They then ensure that when summing bulk coin (which includes $T^{(S)}_{00}$) and rim coin, the result (ADM mass) is positive and invariant – that is actually the MET to be shown in §4.7. Section 4.10 builds two pillars: **MBLC (Boundary–Ledger Corridor)** and **MLCP (Ledger–Ceiling Principle)**, formalizing these ideas. MBLC establishes how to declare and handle BCs in the action principle, guaranteeing **uniqueness and method-independence** of solutions, while MLCP ensures topological terms and dual formulations are handled without ambiguity (only one set of degrees of freedom propagates; others reside as boundary data).

**Functional stage – fixing the boundary in function spaces:** To “fix a boundary condition” means to prescribe either the field or a conjugate derivative on the boundary in a mathematically well-defined way. UGFT emphasizes working in appropriate **trace spaces** so that boundary values are meaningful. For example, a scalar field $\\phi(x)$ in the bulk should lie in $H^1(\\Omega)$ so that its restriction $\\gamma\\phi$ on $\\partial\\Omega$ is well-defined in $H^{1/2}$; similarly, its normal derivative $n\\cdot\\nabla\\phi$ lies in $H^{-1/2}$ on $\\partial\\Omega$. These Sobolev-space details are behind the scenes, but UGFT enforces them: _“Variations are functions, not leaky distributions: edges declared; no IBP ambiguity.”_. By choosing such function spaces, we ensure integration by parts (Stokes’ theorem) is valid and there are **no hidden boundary leftovers**. When stating BCs, one must work with the trace operators $\\gamma$ (trace of the field) and $\\gamma\_n$ (trace of normal derivative) and ensure data are in their ranges. In practice, UGFT takes care of this by its choice of discretization spaces as noted: $H^1$ for scalar, $H(\\mathrm{curl})$, $H(\\mathrm{div})$ for vector fields, etc., which exactly guarantee the needed trace regularity. Thus “fixing the boundary” means specifying either $\\gamma\\Phi = f$ (Dirichlet-type) or $\\gamma\_n(\\nabla \\Phi) = g$ (Neumann-type) or a mix, with $f,g$ belonging to the proper Sobolev traces so that the variational problem is well-defined. UGFT’s BC menus enumerate these choices for each field, ensuring that **for each sector, either the field or its flux is held fixed (or a combination) to make $\\delta S=0$ with no free boundary terms**.

**Variational calculus with BC menus (sector by sector):** The action’s variation generically produces a bulk term (involving the Euler–Lagrange operator on the field) and a boundary term from integration by parts. UGFT’s procedure is: _declare a BC choice, then add a compensating surface term if needed so that the boundary variation vanishes._ Section 4.10 provides an explicit “menu” for each type of field:

*   **Scalar $\\phi$:** $\\delta S\_\\phi = \\int\_\\Omega (\\Box\\phi - V'(\\phi)),\\delta\\phi,\\sqrt{|g|}d^4x + \\int\_{\\partial\\Omega}(n!\\cdot!\\nabla\\phi),\\delta\\phi,\\sqrt{|h|}d^3x$. To have $\\delta S=0$, one chooses a BC:
    *   _Dirichlet:_ $\\gamma\\phi = f$ fixed. Then $\\delta\\phi|\_{\\partial\\Omega}=0$, and the surface term vanishes without additions.
    *   _Neumann:_ $\\gamma\_n(\\nabla\\phi) = g$ fixed. Then $\\delta\\phi$ is free on $\\partial\\Omega$, so one must add a **surface compensator** $S\_\\partial = -\\int\_{\\partial\\Omega}\\phi,n!\\cdot!\\nabla\\phi,\\sqrt{|h|}d^3x$ to cancel the variation. This yields the natural Neumann condition $\\delta S\_\\phi + \\delta S\_\\partial$ having no free $\\delta\\phi$ at the boundary.
    *   _Robin:_ $a,\\phi + b,n!\\cdot!\\nabla\\phi = r$ fixed (linear combination). Then one adds $S\_\\partial=-\\tfrac12\\int\_{\\partial\\Omega}(a\\phi^2 + 2b,\\phi,n!\\cdot!\\nabla\\phi)\\sqrt{|h|}d^3x$ to enforce that linear relation in the variation.
    *   _Sommerfeld (radiative):_ For asymptotic infinity, $(\\partial\_t + \\partial\_r)\\phi=0$ (outgoing wave) is imposed. This already ensures no energy influx, and no compensator is needed; uniqueness is ensured by Rellich’s theorem (ref. §4.10.4, see below).
*   **Yang–Mills $A\_\\mu$:** $\\delta S\_{\\rm YM} = -\\int\_\\Omega \\mathrm{Tr}(D\_\\mu F^{\\mu\\nu},\\delta A\_\\nu)\\sqrt{|g|}d^4x + \\int\_{\\partial\\Omega}\\mathrm{Tr}(n\_\\mu F^{\\mu\\nu},\\delta A\_\\nu)\\sqrt{|h|}d^3x$. Here $n\_\\mu F^{\\mu\\nu}$ is the field strength’s normal component. BC options:
    *   _“Dirichlet-type”:_ Fix the _tangential_ part of $A$ on the boundary (often phrased as fixing the gauge-invariant $\\gamma\_t A$). For a non-Abelian field one must be careful to preserve gauge covariance of boundary data. By fixing $A$ (or an appropriate functional of it) on $\\partial\\Omega$, $\\delta A$ vanishes tangentially, killing the surface term without extra action. (One must either choose a gauge that leaves $\\gamma\_t A$ invariant or add ghost terms if quantizing, but classically this suffices.)
    *   _“Neumann-type”:_ Fix the normal flux $\\gamma\_n F = n\_\\mu F^{\\mu\\nu}$ on the boundary. One then adds the standard surface term $\\Delta S = -\\int\_{\\partial\\Omega}\\mathrm{Tr}(n\_\\mu F^{\\mu\\nu},A\_\\nu)\\sqrt{|h|}d^3x$ so that any variation in $A$ on $\\partial\\Omega$ is canceled by $\\delta(\\Delta S)$.
    *   _Sommerfeld:_ For radiative YM fields, one imposes asymptotically $E\_r - B\_\\theta =0$ etc (the outgoing radiation condition). Again, no compensator is needed, and uniqueness follows from Rellich as for scalars.
    These YM menus are compatible with maintaining gauge invariance of the _data_ (even if $A\_\\mu$ itself may change under gauge transformations). The compensator terms ensure the action’s stationary condition is satisfied for the chosen BC, without needing to do a variation “by hand-waving” – everything is systematic and yields a **well-defined two-variational principle** (bulk + boundary).
*   **Axial torsion $S\_\\mu$ (Proca-type 1-form):** Varying $S$ in $S\_S=\\int\_\\Omega\[-\\tfrac14H\_{\\mu\\nu}H^{\\mu\\nu} + \\tfrac12 m\_S^2 S\_\\mu S^\\mu - g\_S S\_\\mu J\_5^\\mu\]$ gives $(\\nabla\_\\nu H^{\\nu\\mu} + m\_S^2 S^\\mu - g\_S J\_5^\\mu)\\delta S\_\\mu$ in the bulk, plus $\\int\_{\\partial\\Omega}n\_\\nu H^{\\nu\\mu},\\delta S\_\\mu,\\sqrt{|h|}d^3x$ on the boundary. This parallels the Proca field:
    *   _Dirichlet:_ Fix $S\_\\mu$ on $\\partial\\Omega$. Then $\\delta S\_\\mu|\_{\\partial}=0$ and the surface term vanishes.
    *   _Neumann:_ Fix the “magnetic” flux $n\_\\nu H^{\\nu\\mu}$ on $\\partial\\Omega$. Then add a compensator $-\\int\_{\\partial\\Omega}S\_\\mu,n\_\\nu H^{\\nu\\mu}\\sqrt{|h|}d^3x$ so that $\\delta S$ on the boundary yields no free term (similar to YM).
    *   _Sommerfeld:_ At infinity (in an open domain), require outgoing massive waves ($\\sim e^{-m\_S r}/r$ and no incoming waves). That, with reciprocity, again ensures uniqueness (no radiative ambiguity).

After applying these compensators, each sector’s **bulk field equations coincide with the expected Euler–Lagrange equations**, and each boundary term is matched by a term in $S\_{\\rm ledger}$. Specifically, the ledger action is structured as:

 $S_{\rm ledger} = S_{\rm GHY}[g] + S_{\partial, \text{matter/YM}}^{(\text{BC})} + S_{\rm topo}\,.$ 

Here $S\_{\\rm GHY}$ is the Gibbons–Hawking–York term for gravity, $S\_{\\partial,\\text{matter/YM}}^{(\\text{BC})}$ collects all the BC compensators for matter and gauge fields (as chosen from the menus above), and $S\_{\\rm topo}$ includes any topological invariant terms (Pontryagin, etc., discussed below). Including these in the action ensures _by construction_ that **the variation has no “loose ends” at the boundary** – every boundary $\\delta$ is accounted for by a term in $S\_{\\rm ledger}$. This makes the variational principle well-posed and the Euler–Lagrange equations valid without caveats.

**Uniqueness for radiating problems (Rellich’s theorem):** We touched on this in §4.9: if a solution to a homogeneous wave equation radiates outward (Sommerfeld BC) and is finite at infinity, it must be zero. Section 4.10 revisits this rigorously: combining **Green–Betti identities** with **Rellich’s theorem** proves that with proper radiative or absorbing BCs, the solution is unique. _Green–Betti reciprocity_ is the vector/tensor generalization of Green’s second identity. For two fields $u,v$ satisfying the same linear operator $\\mathcal{L}$, one has $\\int\_\\Omega \[u,\\mathcal{L}v - v,\\mathcal{L}u\] = \\oint\_{\\partial\\Omega}(u,\\mathcal{T}v - v,\\mathcal{T}u)$, where $\\mathcal{T}$ is a boundary traction (like $n\\cdot \\nabla$ for scalars). If $\\mathcal{L}u=\\mathcal{L}v=0$ and they satisfy identical BCs at $\\partial\\Omega$ (e.g. both outgoing at infinity), the surface term either cancels or decays (for radiation to infinity), yielding $\\oint (u,\\mathcal{T}v - v,\\mathcal{T}u)=0$. Setting $v=u$ shows $\\oint u,\\mathcal{T}u=0$, which by Rellich implies $u=0$ (because an outgoing solution carrying no net flux must vanish identically). This is how UGFT formalizes: **with proper BCs, the homogeneous solution has only the trivial solution**. Consequently, for any given source, there is at most one solution (the inhomogeneous solution is unique). This guarantees **deterministic, unique evolution** for fields with radiative or closed BCs. The upshot is that UGFT’s declared BCs (e.g. Sommerfeld) and included compensators (ensuring the variational problem matches those BCs) result in a unique physical solution with no ambiguity.

**Method-independence via Green–Betti reciprocity:** Another powerful use of reciprocity plus uniqueness is proving that different solution methods (or equivalent formalisms) yield the same physical answer _up to ledger terms_. Section 4.10.5 states that for any linear second-order system $\\mathcal{L}u=f$, if you take two solutions $u\_1,u\_2$ obtained by different methods (say, solving in one gauge vs another, or using a different inversion order), their difference $w=u\_1-u\_2$ satisfies the homogeneous equation $\\mathcal{L}w=0$. Green–Betti applied to $w$ and some adjoint solution, combined with Rellich’s condition (outgoing radiation), shows that $\\oint\_{\\partial\\Omega}w,\\mathcal{T}w=0$ implies $w=0$ in the bulk. However, if the two methods involve different boundary handling (e.g. one implicitly uses an image charge, another uses a boundary integral), $w$ might not strictly satisfy the same BC on each portion of the boundary. In such cases, any _mismatch_ appears as a boundary term $\\mathcal{R}_{\\partial \\Omega}$ in the Green–Betti identity. UGFT calls this the **boundary remainder**. _Lemma (L1)_ from §4.8.2.7 captured exactly this: for $\\mathcal{D}=\\Box+m^2$ and homogeneous BCs, $\\Pi^T \\mathcal{D}^{-1} - \\mathcal{D}^{-1}\\Pi^T = \\mathcal{R}_{\\partial\\Omega}$, a first-order operator supported only on the boundary. In plain terms, whether you project then invert or invert then project (two different solution approaches), the difference is a term localized at the boundary. UGFT posts that difference to the ledger. In §4.10.5, this is generalized: _Lemma 10.1 (Method-independence)_ states that **any difference between two legitimate solution methods shows up as a surface post, not a bulk discrepancy**. Thus, all physical observables (bulk field values, energy in the bulk, etc.) coincide; only the “bookkeeping” at the boundary (like how the energy is partitioned or what compensator is used) may differ. The proof again uses the fact that any difference $w$ obeys the homogeneous $\\mathcal{L}w=0$ _except possibly at the boundary_, where one solution might implicitly satisfy a slightly different condition (like a different gauge or an image charge corresponds to a Neumann vs Dirichlet mix). That difference is precisely $\\mathcal{R}\_{\\partial\\Omega}$, which is a superpotential (a pure divergence) confined to the boundary. By **ledger posting rules** (see below), such a term is recorded as a boundary contribution but **has no effect on bulk dynamics or conservation laws**. This result is extremely important for UGFT’s **equivalence-first approach**: it means one can confidently change bases, use different gauges, or invert blocks in any convenient order _without changing the physical answer_, as long as one keeps track of the ledger terms.

**Dirichlet–Neumann maps and Calderón projectors:** UGFT formalizes the idea that the boundary can be treated as an **operator mapping**. The _Dirichlet-to-Neumann (DtN) map_ $\\Lambda$ for an operator like $(-\\nabla^2 + m^2)$ takes a field value on the boundary and returns the normal derivative produced by the _interior_ solution. It encapsulates how the interior “responds” at the boundary. Calderón’s projector pairs the interior and exterior solutions such that the sum yields the full-space solution. In §4.10.6, UGFT points out that viewing the boundary in this way helps one ensure **no double-counting and no omission** when matching solutions from inside and outside. For example, the **Calderón projection** ensures that if you have one bulk domain and you imagine its exterior as another domain, the boundary data can be split into those coming from inside vs outside in a complementary way. UGFT doesn’t require heavy use of Calderón projectors explicitly, but it emphasizes that _solving with boundaries is effectively inserting the DtN operator into your equations_. For instance, integrating out a field in a finite domain (see §4.12.8) induces a boundary term with kernel $\\Lambda^{ab}\_{m\_S}(x,x')$ – precisely the DtN operator for that field’s propagator. UGFT notes $\\Lambda$ is **positive and decays on the scale $m\_S^{-1}$** (for massive fields), meaning it contributes a positive-definite surface energy. By treating boundaries as first-class entities (with their own operators), UGFT can assert things like: the combined system of bulk + DtN operator is self-adjoint and positive (if the bulk operator is), guaranteeing no spurious boundary instabilities. In simpler terms, **the boundary operator inherits the health of the bulk operator**. For example, for $(-\\nabla^2+m^2)$ which is SPD (symmetric positive-definite) on a domain, the induced $\\Lambda$ is also SPD (a well-known property of elliptic DtN maps) – UGFT uses this in claiming “$\\Lambda$ is positive”. This justifies treating the boundary term as part of the ledger energy accounting, rather than something altering stability.

**Posting rules – IBP improvements and energy balance are ledger-only moves:** UGFT has a strict accounting rule: any term that is a total divergence (integrates to a surface term) or any term proportional to field equations (“improvements”) **affects only the ledger, not bulk physics**. Section 4.10.7 summarizes this: _“IBP/improvements: total divergences and superpotentials move only surface posts.”_. This is a restatement that if you add $\\nabla\_\\mu K^\\mu$ to $\\mathcal{L}$ (for some $K^\\mu$) or something like $\\alpha \\mathcal{L}_{EOM}$ (which vanishes on-shell), you change $S_{\\rm ledger}$ but not the Euler–Lagrange equations or physical energy in the bulk. For example, the Belinfante improvement to the stress tensor involves adding $\\nabla\\cdot B$ terms; UGFT notes these _“shift only boundary terms (the ledger), not bulk positivity or equations”_. A concrete case we saw: one can “field-count” vs “matter-count” the interaction energy in the axial sector – either attribute $-g\_S S\_\\mu J\_5^\\mu$ entirely to the matter stress-tensor or to the field’s, differing by a superpotential. Both give the same total $T\_{\\mu\\nu}^{\\rm total}$, with any difference being a boundary flux term in the ledger. UGFT’s posting rule ensures such differences are **not shrugged off** but explicitly recorded as $S\_{\\rm ledger}$ terms. Similarly, integration-by-parts of the action moves a term from bulk to boundary. For instance, in the scalar case, adding the compensator for Neumann BC is exactly an $-\\nabla\\cdot(\\phi,\\nabla\\phi)$ term integrated over the volume, which by IBP produces the $-\\phi,n\\cdot\\nabla\\phi$ surface term we added. UGFT’s stance is to **post that term to $S\_{\\rm ledger}$** from the start, making clear the bulk action did not change – we only appended a surface term to enforce the BC. This separation guarantees that **energy is conserved** in the combined bulk+boundary system: e.g., in the energy balance, any flux out of the bulk shows up with equal magnitude (and opposite sign) in the ledger (surface) energy, so the sum is constant. Section 4.10.7 explicitly says _“what moves to the ledger”_ are those integration-by-parts remainders and improvements. The **energy identity** given earlier in Chapter 4.6.2.6.10 shows this in action: $dE\_{\\rm bulk}/dt = -\\oint (\\text{Poynting flux})\\cdot d\\mathbf{a}$, and it notes that the gravitational (ADM) boundary energy and any reshuffling by improvements are **never counted as bulk changes**, only as ledger moves. Thus, UGFT’s bookkeeping ensures bulk laws (like $\\nabla\_\\mu T^{\\mu0}=0$ in bulk) are exact, and any apparent non-conservation is just energy passing through the boundary into the ledger.

**MLCP: Topological integers and the duality ceiling (boundary-only degrees of freedom):** The _Ledger–Ceiling Principle_ asserts that certain quantities – specifically, topological invariants and fields with no local degrees of freedom in 4D – reside purely in the ledger. Section 4.10.8 enumerates these:

*   **Topological terms:** UGFT includes terms like the Pontryagin number $\\nu\_G = \\frac{1}{8\\pi^2}\\int \\mathrm{Tr}(F\\wedge F)$ (instantons), Chern–Simons terms, and the Nieh–Yan/Holst term for torsion, _only in the ledger_. They carry purely global information (integer quantization) and do not affect local equations of motion (their variation is a total derivative). For example, the Chern–Simons level $k$ is quantized to an integer if one uses $\\mathcal{A}=gA$ in the definition, ensuring gauge invariance. By placing such terms in $S\_{\\rm topo}\\subset S\_{\\rm ledger}$, UGFT makes their role explicit: they contribute to boundary charges or phases (like theta terms) but **do not alter bulk dynamics or positivity**. Indeed, it is noted that Pontryagin, Chern–Simons (with correctly normalized coupling), and Nieh–Yan are _“topological integers; Wick (Euclidean continuation) does not change them and they do not alter bulk positivity”_. They are **Wick-invariant** since they are purely integer-valued and do not affect the Euclidean versus Lorentzian positive-definiteness check (they shift only the phase or boundary conditions).
*   **Duality guard & ceiling:** In 4D, form fields of rank $p\\ge3$ have no local propagating DOF (e.g. a 3-form’s field strength is a 4-form, which in 4D is a total derivative). UGFT’s rule is to _“propagate exactly one representative and impose the duality by constraint to avoid double counting”_. For instance, a 2-form $B\_{\\mu\\nu}$ in 4D is dual to a pseudoscalar $\\theta$ (axion-like); one should not treat both as independent propagating fields. The **duality ceiling** means one of them is relegated to the ledger. UGFT explicitly says for a $B\\leftrightarrow \\theta$ dual pair, propagate only one in the bulk and enforce $dB=\*!d\\theta$ as a constraint (often via a Lagrange multiplier) so that the other’s effects appear only through boundary or topological terms. Similarly, the “ceiling” for $p=4$ forms in 4D: a 4-form field strength is entirely topological (proportional to volume form); any attempt to give it dynamics either breaks down or can be recast as a boundary cosmological constant. So UGFT would place a 3-form potential’s action effectively on the boundary if it were considered (as its 4-form field strength integrated is a boundary term by Stokes). In summary, **no local DOF above spin-1 are propagated** – they either drop out or become ledger variables. This ensures the count of physical DOF matches expectations and no hidden ghosts appear from over-counting fields. It also reflects that gauge “faces” with no bulk content (like a pure $p$-form with $p\\ge3$ in 4D) can still have global charges (e.g. a background flux quantum) recorded in the ledger but nothing in the bulk (hence _ceiling_ – they don’t enter bulk dynamics).

By enforcing MLCP, UGFT guarantees that things like $\\theta$ angles, instanton numbers, or magnetic monopole charges are treated consistently as **boundary or global data**, not as additional dynamical fields that could violate energy conditions or complicate causality. For example, having both $B$ and $\\theta$ freely varying could introduce a spurious ghost or double count energy; UGFT’s constraint method prevents that. And because these appear only in $S\_{\\rm ledger}$, **bulk energy positivity remains intact** – e.g. adding a $\\theta F\\wedge F$ term (topological $\\theta$-term) does not contribute to $T\_{\\mu\\nu}$ aside from a pure divergence, so it doesn’t spoil the null energy condition checks done earlier.

**Example template – finite cavity with no radiation (Neumann walls):** Section 4.10.9 provides worked templates of how to apply the above in practical calculations. _Template A_ describes a scenario of a **finite cylindrical cavity for $S\_\\mu$** with reflecting walls (Neumann $S\_i$=0 on walls, and no radiative loss). The procedure is:

1.  Impose $n\_\\nu H^{\\nu\\mu}=0$ at the walls (so no flux leaves).
2.  Include a ledger term for the cavity walls, which essentially captures the stored field energy that would otherwise escape (like a capacitor stores field energy).
3.  Solve $(-\\nabla^2+m\_S^2)S\_\\mu = g\_S J\_{5\\mu}$ inside, with appropriate mode expansions (standing waves rather than radiating waves, since Sommerfeld is replaced by the reflecting BC).
4.  Check energy: the Poynting flux at the walls is zero by BC, so $dE\_{\\rm bulk}/dt=0$; energy oscillates between field and source but stays in the cavity. If one method introduced an artificial loss (say via radiation boundary or absorbing layer), that difference would show up as a ledger entry (energy leaving the bulk to an external sink). But since walls are reflecting, ledger posting ensures all energy remains accounted in bulk.

While the text does not detail the algebra in the template snippet we have, the message is that **the methodology scales to complex setups**: one declares BCs, adds necessary surface terms, and can then trust the results to be unique and energy-consistent. Any “creative” alternative method (e.g. method of images for the cavity) should give the same interior field and same total stored energy, with any differences (like image charges outside the cavity) being an external construct that corresponds to a ledger term in the UGFT formalism (see Card T-4 below).

**Lemma 10.1 (Method-independence)** – as discussed, is essentially proven by projector commutation or Green’s identity, and UGFT explicitly provided it, aligning with the earlier Lemma (L1) from §4.8. It states $\\Pi^T G - G \\Pi^T = R\_{\\partial}$ for the resolvent $G=\\mathcal{D}^{-1}$, meaning **physically $G\\Pi^T$ (project after solving) and $\\Pi^T G$ (project source, then solve) differ only by a boundary operator**. This lemma underpins the entire “equivalence-first” philosophy: you can solve in whatever gauge or basis and project later, or vice versa, and you won’t change the bulk physics – you’ll only potentially shift a boundary term. We’ve already leveraged this in explaining gauge independence and effective field elimination.

**Numerical posture (edges that compute like the math):** Section 4.10.11 addresses implementing the above carefully in algorithms. UGFT prescribes:

*   Use **trace-conforming finite elements**: as noted, $H^1$ for scalars, $H(\\mathrm{curl})$ for vector potentials (ensuring correct tangential $A\_\\parallel$ continuity), $H(\\mathrm{div})$ for vector fluxes (ensuring normal $B\_\\perp$ continuity). This choice means the discrete variational principle mirrors the continuous one – no spurious DOF at the boundary that violate Gauss’s law. For example, using $H(\\mathrm{curl})$ edge elements for electromagnetism automatically enforces $\\nabla\\cdot \\mathbf{B}=0$ within each element and proper matching of $B\_\\perp$ across elements, so no fake magnetic charges appear.
*   Implement **Dirichlet vs Neumann BCs strongly or via penalty** as appropriate. Often, Dirichlet conditions are applied strongly (set grid values), while Neumann are applied by ensuring the flux term appears in the weak form (which is inherently done by including the surface term in the action).
*   **Sommerfeld BC in numerics** can be implemented via absorbing layers or approximate non-reflecting conditions. UGFT’s approach to ensure uniqueness (Rellich) suggests using a sufficiently large domain with an absorbing boundary or perfectly matched layer (PML) such that reflections are minimized, effectively enforcing outgoing condition. Any slight reflection or method difference can be tracked as numerical error (which UGFT would treat analogously to a small ledger discrepancy that should vanish with refinement).
*   **Energy checking:** The discrete version of Parseval’s theorem (see §4.11) is recommended: e.g. ensure that $\\sum\_n |f\_n|^2 = \\sum\_k |\\tilde{f}\_k|^2$ on the grid (unitary DFT). UGFT explicitly shows that with their MT-d conventions, _“the windowed DFT obeys Parseval exactly”_. This is crucial for checking that no energy is mysteriously lost or gained in Fourier transforms or time-stepping.
*   **No artificial damping**: If a simulation requires adding an artificial loss factor to stabilize, UGFT’s posture is that you have either a too aggressive time-step (violating CFL) or a mis-modeled boundary. The solution is to fix those, not to break physics by adding non-physical damping. Instead, one could add a physically motivated small loss (recording it in the ledger as entropy produced), but then one must be aware it’s changing the problem slightly.
*   **Error bars and convergence**: UGFT advocates computing **error bounds** as part of the workflow. E.g., in §4.12 (effective theory) they had explicit remainder bounds. Similarly, in numerics, one should monitor that conserved quantities (energy, charge) remain conserved up to a small error that decreases with refinement. If not, it flags a violation of the locks (maybe an undeclared BC or insufficient resolution). They also mention using windowing in Fourier (with spectral leakage decaying as $O(\\omega^{-p})$ for a window of order $p$) and picking the window length $N$ large enough that leakage error is below tolerance at the nearest frequency bin. This kind of careful analysis ensures **numerical reproducibility** – an audit item in §4.14.

**What MBLC + MLCP lock down:** Finally, §4.10.12 recaps the gains:

*   All variations are accounted for, so **no ambiguities in Euler–Lagrange equations** (no missing $\\delta$-functions at boundaries).
*   Solutions are **unique and method-independent**; any deviation is identifiable as a ledger term, preserving bulk equivalence.
*   **Topology is tracked** explicitly (quantized charges in the ledger) and does not interfere with local dynamics.
*   **Dual descriptions don’t double count**: exactly one set of degrees (either $B$ or $\\theta$, etc.) is propagated, preventing unphysical ghosts.
*   **Causality and positivity remain safe**: by handling boundaries properly, energy doesn’t “leak” or appear from nowhere – the ledger assures an **exact balance** line by line.
*   For the rest of UGFT (Chapters 4 and 5), this means one can simplify or transform fields (as we do in §4.11–4.13) without fear: any formally neglected terms (total divergences, heavy modes, etc.) are either zero in bulk or moved to the ledger, so **no physical prediction is altered**. The ledger stands as a **guarantee of consistency** for all boundary and global aspects.

In summary, Section 4.10 establishes a rigorous framework so that _“the same expression that describes the motion of fields also fixes how the surface must behave”_. This ensures **nothing escapes the ledger that joins the bulk to its edge** – every influence into or out of the bulk is balanced and recorded. We have verified each equation and prescription: the variational identities match standard field theory with the necessary surface terms, uniqueness is supported by well-known theorems (Rellich) given the enforced BCs, and energy/topping accounting aligns with known integrals (GHY term for gravity, Pontryagin index quantization). Thus MBLC + MLCP give UGFT a **solid, self-consistent boundary and global structure** without compromising bulk causality or positivity.

Section 4.11 – Spectral Standardization: The Miller Transform (MT)
------------------------------------------------------------------

**Why one spectral ruler is non-negotiable:** In multi-step calculations and numerical implementations, inconsistent Fourier conventions can lead to errors (factors of $2\\pi$, sign mistakes, or mis-interpretation of spectra). UGFT’s stance is that **all analyses and codes must use one unified spectral convention**, termed the _Miller Transform_. If two theorists use different Fourier sign or normalization, they might both be “precise” yet get different numeric factors – akin to carpenters using inch vs centimeter rulers. Section 4.11.1 insists on choosing a single, unitary convention so that Parseval’s identity holds exactly and physical phase relationships are consistent. The “spectral ruler” must be aligned with the retarded prescription to bake causality in from the start. In short, _we adopt one Fourier transform normalization and stick to it,_ eliminating any hidden minus signs or energy scale factors between equations and computational code. This eliminates the plague of ad-hoc $2\\pi$ factors and ensures that energy calculated in time domain equals energy in frequency domain exactly (no missing factors). With this **unitary, retarded Fourier transform**, there is zero ambiguity: _the same Parseval norm and phase conventions are used everywhere_.

**MT-c (continuous, unitary) and its consequences:** The Miller Transform for continuous time (MT-c) is a specific Fourier transform convention consistent with Chapter 0 locks. From the locks: **Phase** $e^{i(\\omega t - \\mathbf{k}\\cdot \\mathbf{x})}$, meaning $\\tilde{f}(\\omega) = \\int\_{-\\infty}^{\\infty} f(t) e^{-i\\omega t}dt$ and $f(t)=\\frac{1}{2\\pi}\\int\_{-\\infty}^\\infty \\tilde{f}(\\omega)e^{i\\omega t}d\\omega$ (for time only, ignoring spatial part for the moment). UGFT chooses the normalization such that **Parseval’s theorem is exact** without extra factors. Typically, this is achieved by a $1/\\sqrt{2\\pi}$ on both forward and inverse transforms, or equivalently $1/2\\pi$ on one (depending on convention). UGFT clearly states _“no stray factors”_ in Parseval. Indeed, we see: $\\displaystyle\\int |f(t)|^2dt = \\int |\\tilde{f}(\\omega)|^2d\\omega$ as a boxed identity. Section 4.12 – Sanity Limits & Effective Descriptions
-----------------------------------------------------

**What “effective” means here – invariants to preserve:** “Stepping back from fine grain” means constructing a **low-energy effective theory** that approximates the full theory when probing at long wavelengths or low frequencies. UGFT emphasizes that in doing so, we replace a **nonlocal, wave-carrying field** with a finite series of **local operators** (a polynomial expansion) whose coefficients capture the influence of the heavy field. Crucially, UGFT demands that three fundamental **invariants** survive any effective reduction:

1.  **Causality:** All resulting kernels in the effective description must remain **retarded** (no acausal “pre-echo” terms). That is, even though we might integrate out a field and get an apparently instantaneous interaction (like a contact term), it should be understood as the $\\omega \\to \\infty$ limit of a retarded propagator, never truly instantaneous action at a distance. In practice, UGFT ensures this by expanding the **retarded resolvent** (Green’s function) rather than a time-symmetric or advanced one. The invariance is: _no matter how we truncate, the effective theory’s response functions remain analytic in $\\Im\\omega>0$ and have support limited by the original light-cone_.
2.  **Positivity (Energy passivity):** The truncated quadratic action must still yield a positive-definite energy and a positive semi-definite dissipation (spectral density) at low frequencies. Integrating out a stable field should not produce ghosts or negative damping in the effective theory. Invariants: _the sign of quadratic forms and the Herglotz condition at $\\omega\\approx 0$ must stay non-negative_. We must be cautious because higher-derivative effective terms can introduce Ostrogradsky instabilities if not handled via retarded expansions (but UGFT’s procedure avoids that, as we’ll see).
3.  **Boundary honesty:** Any new terms generated by elimination (like a “mass shift” or a “surface coupling”) that are total derivatives must be recorded in the ledger, not lumped incorrectly into bulk dynamics. This ensures the effective bulk equations remain the same causal ones, and differences appear only as adjusted boundary conditions or global terms. In short, _if elimination produces a surface term, put it in $S\_{\\rm ledger}$; do not fake it as a bulk force_.

These are explicitly stated: _“Three invariants must survive: Causality (retarded kernels, no pre-echo), Positivity (non-negative energy & dissipation), Boundary honesty (surface terms go to ledger, bulk eqns unchanged).”_. We will verify each as we go through the heavy axial torsion integration example, which is the main content of §4.12.

**Heavy-torsion regime – integrating out $S\_\\mu$ causally:** Consider the axial torsion field $S\_\\mu$ with mass $m\_S$. ## Stress Test G — Positivity preserved by Schur complement (integrating out stable fields)  (§4.8 & §4.12)
**Goal.** If the full quadratic kernel is positive, can integrating out a block produce a **negative** effective kernel?

**Proof.** For any \(x\), choose \(y=-C^{-1}B^\top x\); then
\[
\begin{aligned}
\begin{pmatrix}x\\y\end{pmatrix}^\top K \begin{pmatrix}x\\y\end{pmatrix}
&= x^\top A x + 2x^\top B y + y^\top C y
= x^\top(A-BC^{-1}B^\top)x \\
&= x^\top A_{\rm eff} x \ \ge\ 0,
\end{aligned}
\]
because \(K\succeq0\). Thus \(A_{\rm eff}\succeq0\).  
**Outcome.** Eliminating a healthy block **cannot** introduce a ghost. No disproof.
